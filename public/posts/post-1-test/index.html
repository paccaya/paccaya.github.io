In a world perpetually filled with noise, whether that be light pollution, sound pollution, or the cantankerous humdrum of thoughts and overbearing worries and dreams, our senses seemed to be bombarded with information, entertainment, concepts, language, and more generally sound. With the Internet as our nexus, we appear to be so hyperconnected on paper while simultaneously, and rather abhorrently, we are also dealing with an epidemic of anxiety, repression, dis-ease, and loneliness, especially amongst adolescents.

As we know, appearances are deceptively buoyant and act as floation devices in a sea of entropy: concepts only correspond to reality so much so as codified symbols and myths that we use to explain and tell different stories. May it be neuropsychiatry, applied Bayesian statistics, ecopsychology, quantam physics or the Myth of Icarus, these all can be classified as a story in the sense that it allows our minds to make sense of the world around us. Some may be based on rational, scientific methodology while others may be simple speculation, fiction, and fantasy—both being equally valuable and often as true as the beholder wants them to see.

My friend Holley Humphrey identified some common behaviors that prevent us from being sufficiently present to connect empathetically with others. The following are examples: Advising: "I think you should ..." "How come you didn't ...?" One-upping: "That's nothing; wait'll you hear what happened to me." Educating: "This could turn into a very positive experience for you if you just ..." Consoling: "It wasn't your fault; you did the best you could." Story-telling: "This reminds me of the time ..." Shutting down: "Cheer up. Don't feel so bad." Sympathizing: "Oh, you poor thing ..." Interrogating: "When did this begin?"1

Receving anothers feelings through the bounds of an ego is akin to fighting fire with fire: one doesn't listen by spewing out things the one's ego is already capable of (see Figure 1 for a reminder). However, it is by healing anothers emotional wounds by simply letting them out: to suffer with, which is what it means to compassionate (Latin com- and pati, meaning to bear or suffer and also to permit or allow). Ego is fundamentally treated only through non-ego since once the emotional cloud has passed: the person is thus healed. In a less abstract sense, receiving empathetically is allowing oneself to be an extension of another person; to find security outside the trap of the isolated, egotistic mind.

In an even more limpid fashion, there is a Buddhist saying often attributed to Thich Nhat Hanh:
"Often we tell ourselves, 'Don't just sit there, do something!' But when we practice awareness [In this case, empathetically listening], we discover that the opposite may be more helpful: 'Don't just do something, sit there!'" "
